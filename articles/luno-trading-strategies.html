Luno Trading Strategies: Momentum & Volume

March 30, 2026

Last updated: Updating…

Luno is one of the most familiar crypto exchanges for South Africans, which makes it a natural place for beginners to start trading. But familiarity can create false confidence. Many traders think a clean mobile app means easy profits. In practice, Luno trading still requires structure, risk control, and patience.

This guide focuses on a realistic approach built around momentum and volume. Those two ideas are simple enough for beginners to understand, but useful enough that more experienced traders still rely on them every day. The goal is not to predict every move. It is to identify better setups, avoid obvious traps, and protect capital when the market turns.

Why momentum matters on Luno

Momentum trading means participating when price is already moving with strength. Instead of trying to guess the exact bottom, you wait for evidence that buyers are taking control. On Luno, this can be especially helpful because many ZAR pairs move in bursts. A coin may trade quietly for hours and then suddenly expand on volume when attention arrives.

The advantage of momentum trading is that you are trading with the market instead of arguing with it. The disadvantage is that late entries can become expensive very quickly. That is why confirmation matters.

Why volume is the confirmation tool

Price can lie for short periods. Volume often tells a clearer story. If a coin breaks above resistance on weak volume, there is a decent chance the move fails. If the same breakout happens with volume meaningfully above recent averages, the move deserves more respect.

A practical way to think about volume on Luno is to compare current participation to recent participation. If current activity is noticeably stronger than the average of the last several candles or hours, that suggests fresh interest rather than random drift.

The core setup: breakout plus volume

  1. Find a coin that has been consolidating below a clear resistance level.
  2. Wait for price to break above that level.
  3. Confirm that volume expands during or just before the breakout.
  4. Enter only if the risk still makes sense relative to your stop level.

That last point is where many traders go wrong. Even a valid breakout can become a poor trade if price has already run too far. A good setup does not excuse a bad entry.

How to mark support and resistance properly

You do not need perfect lines. You need useful zones. Resistance is simply an area where price has struggled to move higher before. Support is an area where buyers previously stepped in. When price finally escapes a range, that old resistance often becomes the first area traders watch on a retest.

On Luno, simple chart reading usually works better than trying to overload the chart with indicators. If you can clearly see a range and clearly see price leaving that range with rising activity, you already have something valuable.

How fees and slippage change real results

Many beginner articles ignore one of the most important trading realities: your setup is not your final result. Execution matters. Luno traders need to respect spreads, fees, and slippage. A setup that looks attractive on paper can lose its edge if you enter with a market order into a fast-moving candle.

That is why limit orders are usually safer when liquidity is thinner or when the market is moving quickly. Good trading is not only about direction. It is also about execution quality.

Example: what a good breakout looks like

Imagine TRX/ZAR trades below a visible ceiling for several sessions. Price tests the same resistance multiple times but cannot close above it. Then a new move starts. Volume rises sharply, price closes above the level, and follow-through continues into the next candle. That is the kind of breakout traders want to study.

In that situation, a disciplined trader might avoid chasing the first spike and instead look for either a calm continuation entry or a retest of the breakout area. The key is that the market has shown evidence of real buying pressure rather than a random pop.

Example: what a fakeout looks like

Now imagine the same coin briefly trades above resistance, but volume remains ordinary, price stalls immediately, and the candle closes back below the breakout zone. That is a classic warning sign. Traders who buy every breakout without checking volume often get trapped by this exact pattern.

One of the best habits a Luno trader can learn is to ask: “Did this move attract real participation, or did it just touch a level?” That question alone filters out many bad trades.

When momentum continuation works

Not every good trade is a quick scalp. Sometimes the strongest moves continue longer than expected. Momentum continuation works best when:

  • volume stays elevated after the breakout,
  • price keeps forming higher lows,
  • there is a clear catalyst or positive market narrative, and
  • broader market conditions are not working against the coin.

In these cases, exiting too early can be as damaging as entering too late. A trailing stop or a rules-based scale-out plan can help manage that problem.

How to manage exits more professionally

Before entering, decide what would prove your trade idea wrong. That is your invalidation point. Your stop-loss should make sense in relation to that structure, not just because someone online said 3% is a good number.

Good exit planning usually includes:

  • an invalidation level where the trade thesis is broken,
  • a profit-taking plan for partial or full exits, and
  • a trailing method if the trend remains strong.

The better your exit rules, the less emotional your decisions become while the trade is live.

Common mistakes South African Luno traders make

  • Buying after a huge candle: by then, the reward may no longer justify the risk.
  • Ignoring volume: price alone is not enough.
  • Using oversized positions: one bad trade should not damage your account badly.
  • Trading every coin: focus on the cleanest setups instead of trying to catch everything.
  • Holding and hoping: when the setup fails, hope is not a strategy.

Who this style suits best

Momentum and volume trading generally suits traders who like structure, can wait for confirmation, and do not need to be in the market all the time. It is less suitable for traders who constantly chase excitement, ignore stops, or make decisions based on social media sentiment alone.

A simple process to follow

  1. Watch a small list of coins rather than everything.
  2. Mark obvious support and resistance levels.
  3. Wait for a clean move with stronger-than-usual volume.
  4. Enter only if the stop placement still makes sense.
  5. Manage the trade with a clear exit plan.
  6. Record what happened so you can improve.

Bottom line

Luno trading does not need to be complicated, but it does need to be disciplined. Momentum gives you direction. Volume gives you confirmation. Risk management keeps you in the game when the market inevitably proves you wrong.
