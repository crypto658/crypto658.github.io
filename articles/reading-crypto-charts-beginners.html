How to Read Crypto Trading Charts – A SA Beginner's Guide

April 29, 2026

Last updated: April 29, 2026

Every successful South African crypto trader started exactly where you are now — staring at a chart full of lines, candles, and numbers that look like a foreign language. The good news: reading crypto charts is a learnable skill, and you don't need a finance degree to master it. This guide will take you from confused to confident, using Luno's charts as our practical reference point.

The goal isn't to become a professional analyst. It's to understand what you're actually looking at when you open Luno, and to make decisions based on evidence rather than guesswork or tips from WhatsApp groups.

Why Learning to Read Charts Beats Following Tips

South Africa has no shortage of crypto signals groups on WhatsApp and Telegram. Many promise "guaranteed gains" or "next big pump" alerts. Almost all of them lose money for followers over time. Why? Because tips without context are worthless — you don't know when to buy, when to take profit, or when to cut a loss.

Reading charts gives you something much more valuable than a tip: independence. You can evaluate any coin yourself, spot opportunities before they become obvious, and understand when a "hot tip" is actually a dangerous pump-and-dump. That skill compounds over your entire investing career.

The Basic Setup – What You're Looking At

Open Luno on your phone or browser and open the BTC/ZAR trading pair. You'll see a chart with a line or candlesticks moving over time. Here's what each element means:

The timeline (x-axis): The horizontal axis shows time. You can zoom in to see minute-by-minute price action, or zoom out to see days, weeks, or months. In Luno, you switch timeframes using the tabs above the chart — 15m, 1h, 4h, 1D, 1W.

The price scale (y-axis): The vertical axis shows the price in ZAR. On Luno's BTC/ZAR chart, this currently ranges from roughly R1.8M at the bottom to over R2.5M at the top, depending on the time period selected.

The candlesticks: Each candle represents a period of price action. A green (or white) candle means the price went up during that period. A red (or black) candle means the price went down. The body of the candle shows the open and close price; the wicks (thin lines above and below) show the highest and lowest prices reached.

Candlesticks – The Language of Price

Candlesticks tell you the story of a trading period in seconds. A long green candle with a short wick tells you buyers dominated — the price opened near the low and closed near the high. A small body with long wicks on both sides tells you the price was rejected in both directions, suggesting uncertainty.

Some patterns are particularly important for SA crypto traders:

Doji candles have almost no body — open and close are nearly identical. This signals indecision and often appears at key turning points. If you see a doji after a prolonged upward move, it can signal that momentum is weakening.

Bullish engulfing candles appear when a small red candle is followed by a large green candle that completely covers the previous candle's body. This is a signal that buyers have taken control — useful when you're deciding whether to enter a long position.

Shooting star candles have a small body at the bottom and a long upper wick. The price tried to push higher but was rejected. If this appears at a resistance level, it's a strong warning that the price might soon fall.

Support and Resistance – Finding Your Entry Points

Support and resistance are the most important concepts in chart reading, and they're genuinely intuitive once you understand them.

Support is a price level where buying pressure has historically been strong enough to stop the price from falling further. Think of it as a floor. When BTC/ZAR dropped to around R1.98M in early 2026 and bounced back up, that level became a support zone.

Resistance is a ceiling — a level where selling pressure has historically stopped the price from rising. For BTC/ZAR in mid-2026, R2.25M has been acting as a resistance level across multiple attempts.

The way to find these levels on Luno is simple: look for price zones where the chart has reversed multiple times. Each time the price bounces off a level, that level becomes more significant. The more times a level is tested, the stronger it tends to be — until eventually it breaks.

When a support level breaks, it often becomes a new resistance level (and vice versa). This is one of the most reliable patterns in technical analysis. If BTC/ZAR breaks below R1.98M with strong volume, that former support becomes a new ceiling — a signal to be more cautious about buying.

Volume – The Most Underrated Indicator

Volume is the amount of trading happening in a given period. It's displayed as bars at the bottom of the Luno chart. High volume confirms price movements; low volume suggests the move might not last.

Here's the practical application: when BTC/ZAR breaks through a resistance level on high volume — say 1.5× or 2× the average volume — that's a much more reliable signal than a break on thin volume. The high volume means many traders are actively buying at that level, which creates stronger support for the new price.

On Luno, you can set a volume alert by drawing a line at the average volume level. When volume spikes above it, you know something significant is happening in the market — worth checking the news to see what's driving it.

South African traders often underestimate volume. But for Luno's BTC/ZAR pair, where liquidity is genuine and real orders are being placed, volume is one of the most reliable secondary indicators available.

RSI – Identifying Overbought and Oversold Conditions

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum indicator shown as a line graph below the main chart, ranging from 0 to 100. In Luno, you add it via the Indicators menu.

The key levels are 30 and 70. When RSI drops below 30, the asset is considered oversold — sellers may have overextended, and a bounce is possible. When RSI rises above 70, it's overbought — buyers may have overextended, and the price could soon pull back.

For BTC/ZAR in 2026, we've seen RSI drop into oversold territory around 32–35 during the February correction, followed by a bounce. Conversely, during the November 2025 pump when BTC hit R2.4M, RSI pushed above 75 — a clear overbought signal that preceded a healthy correction.

Important caution: RSI works best in range-bound markets. In strong trends, RSI can stay overbought or oversold for extended periods. Don't short just because RSI says "overbought" in a powerful bull market — use it as one piece of evidence among several.

A Real Example – Reading a BTC/ZAR Chart on Luno

Let's walk through a recent practical example on Luno. In March 2026, BTC/ZAR was trading in a range between R1.98M (support) and R2.25M (resistance). Here's how the chart read:

Step 1: The price approached R2.25M resistance. RSI climbed above 68 — not yet overbought, but getting elevated.

Step 2: On March 14th, BTC/ZAR attempted to break R2.25M on volume that was actually below average. The break failed and the price retreated. The chart told you the move wasn't supported by real buying pressure.

Step 3: The price dropped back to R2.05M, well above the R1.98M support level. RSI hit 38 — oversold but not yet at extreme levels. Support was holding.

Step 4: Two days later, volume picked up and BTC/ZAR bounced from R2.05M back toward R2.15M. The support zone held.

The lesson: the chart told you the first breakout attempt was weak before it happened. Volume was below average, RSI was elevated, and the price couldn't sustain above R2.25M. Anyone watching the chart had a clear reason to be cautious — not because of a tip, but because the evidence was there in the data.

Common Chart Patterns Every SA Trader Should Know

Double top: The price tests a resistance level twice, fails to break it, and then drops. For BTC/ZAR, if the price tested R2.4M twice in April 2026 without breaking it, that would be a double top pattern — a bearish signal.

Head and shoulders: A peak (left shoulder), followed by a higher peak (head), then a lower peak (right shoulder). The pattern suggests a trend reversal from bullish to bearish. This is one of the most reliable reversal patterns in technical analysis.

Ascending triangle: The price forms a horizontal resistance level while making higher lows — creating a triangle shape. A break above the horizontal line signals continuation of the uptrend. Useful for identifying breakout opportunities on Luno.

Descending wedge: A tightening pattern where the price makes lower highs and lower lows. Wedges can break in either direction, but a descending wedge in a downtrend often signals a coming upward reversal.

Building Your Chart Reading Practice

The best way to learn chart reading is to do it daily — not for long periods, but consistently. Spend five minutes each morning checking the BTC/ZAR chart on Luno: note the current price, whether it's trending up or down, where RSI is sitting, and what the volume looks like compared to average. Over a few weeks, you'll start developing an intuition for normal versus abnormal patterns.

Keep a simple journal. Write down what you observe and what you think will happen. A week later, check whether your prediction was correct and what the chart actually showed. This is how experience compounds — not from tips, but from building your own pattern recognition over time.

The goal is to get to a point where you can look at Luno's BTC/ZAR chart and immediately see what's happened in the last week — whether the price is in a range, trending up or down, and where the key support and resistance levels sit. That's a learnable skill that pays dividends for your entire crypto journey.
