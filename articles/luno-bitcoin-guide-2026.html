How to Buy Bitcoin on Luno in 2026 – Complete South African Guide

April 15, 2026

Last updated: Loading...

South Africa's cryptocurrency landscape has matured significantly, and Luno remains the dominant platform for new Bitcoin buyers in the country. With over 3 million users across sub-Saharan Africa and full FSCA oversight as a licensed crypto asset service provider, Luno offers the most accessible on-ramp for South Africans wanting to enter the Bitcoin market. This guide walks you through everything you need to know to buy your first Bitcoin on Luno in 2026, from account verification to executing your first trade and securing your holdings.

Why Luno Remains the Most Popular Choice for SA Investors

Luno launched in South Africa in 2013 and has built a reputation that distinguishes it from international platforms. The exchange operates under the Financial Sector Conduct Authority's (FSCA) regulatory framework, which means your ZAR deposits are protected under South African financial consumer laws. Unlike offshore exchanges that may block SA users or complicate ZAR withdrawals, Luno's bank accounts sit with South African institutions including First National Bank and Standard Bank, making deposits and withdrawals straightforward for local users.

The platform's mobile-first approach resonates strongly with South African users, many of whom access financial services primarily through smartphones. Luno's app has consistently ranked among the top financial apps on the Google Play Store in South Africa, and its local customer support team operates during South African business hours — something offshore platforms rarely provide. The integration with local payment rails means you can deposit money via EFT from any major South African bank without currency conversion headaches or international wire fees.

Setting Up Your Luno Account – FICA Requirements in 2026

Before you can buy Bitcoin on Luno, you need a verified account. South Africa's Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act requires cryptocurrency platforms to conduct customer due diligence, commonly known as FICA (Financial Intelligence Centre Act) compliance. Luno requires every user to complete identity verification before trading or depositing significant amounts.

The account creation process starts at luno.com or through the Luno app available on iOS and Android. You'll be asked for your South African mobile number (used for two-factor authentication), your full legal name as it appears on your identity document, your South African ID number, and a valid email address. The process asks you to agree to Luno's terms of service and privacy policy, which you should read carefully — particularly the sections on how your data is stored and the platform's approach to dispute resolution.

Identity verification requires you to upload a clear photo of your South African ID (green barcoded ID book or the new smart card), a selfie for liveness verification, and proof of address if you're depositing over R25,000. Proof of address can be a recent utility bill, bank statement, or retail account statement dated within the last three months. Luno's automated verification system usually processes standard applications within 15 minutes during business hours, though the Verification Team may manually review accounts where the system cannot confidently match the submitted documents.

Once verified, you unlock the full trading experience — including the ability to deposit ZAR, buy Bitcoin, and withdraw back to your South African bank account. Unverified accounts can receive Bitcoin but cannot trade or withdraw ZAR, which makes completing verification essential from day one.

Depositing South African Rand via EFT

The most common way to fund a Luno account is via Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT) from your South African bank account. Luno supports deposits from FNB, ABSA, Standard Bank, Nedbank, Capitec, and TymeBank. Each bank has slightly different processing times, but EFT deposits from the big four banks (FNB, ABSA, Standard Bank, Nedbank) typically clear within 1-2 business hours during standard banking hours.

To deposit, navigate to the "Wallet" section in the Luno app or website and select "ZAR" and then "Deposit." Luno will generate a unique reference number that you must use when making the EFT from your online banking portal. This reference is critical — it ties your deposit to your specific Luno account. Without the correct reference, your funds may be held in a suspense account and could take several days to reconcile manually.

For FNB users specifically, Luno deposits can be initiated through FNB's Online Banking platform under "Payments" → "Add Beneficiary" → "Luno." The beneficiary bank is FNB, and you'll need the account number provided on Luno's deposit screen along with your personalised reference. ABSA users can similarly add Luno as a beneficiary through the ABSA Online portal, while Standard Bank customers use their Standard Bank online platform or mobile app.

Minimum deposit amounts on Luno are R50 for EFT transfers. There is no maximum deposit limit per transaction, but Luno applies a daily and monthly trading limit based on your account verification tier. Basic verified accounts typically have a R10,000 daily limit and R50,000 monthly limit, while fully upgraded accounts can trade significantly more after additional documentation is provided.

Buying Your First Bitcoin – Market Orders vs Limit Orders

Once your ZAR deposit reflects in your Luno ZAR wallet (you'll receive a push notification or email confirmation), you can buy Bitcoin. On Luno's trading screen, you'll be presented with the current BTC/ZAR market price sourced from their order book. For new investors, the Instant Buy feature provides the simplest experience — it executes immediately at the current market price and is analogous to buying at the listed retail rate.

However, more cost-conscious investors should use the Limit Order feature. A limit order allows you to specify the price at which you want to buy Bitcoin. For example, if the current BTC/ZAR price is R2,150,000 and you believe the price is likely to pull back before rising further, you could place a limit buy order at R2,100,000. If Bitcoin drops to that level, your order fills automatically. Limit orders on Luno execute as maker orders, which attract lower fees than taker orders placed at market price.

The distinction between market orders and limit orders matters because of Luno's fee structure. Taker orders — which include Instant Buy and any market order that immediately matches against the order book — carry a 1.5% fee on Luno's platform. Maker orders, placed as limit orders that add liquidity to the order book, carry a reduced fee of 0.25%. For a R10,000 Bitcoin purchase, that difference is R125 in fees — a meaningful amount that compounds over multiple trades. Active traders should always prefer limit orders when possible.

Understanding Luno's Fee Structure

Luno's fees can catch new users off guard if they don't read the fine print. The platform charges fees at two levels: deposit fees and trading fees. EFT deposits are free for most users, though some banks may charge their own transfer fees. Credit and debit card deposits carry a 3.5% processing fee, making them significantly more expensive than EFT and something to avoid unless you need immediate funding.

Trading fees, as mentioned above, are tiered: 1.5% for takers, 0.25% for makers. For withdrawals to your South African bank account, Luno charges a R10 fee for Standard EFT withdrawals and R35 for immediate withdrawals. The R10 standard EFT is the most cost-effective withdrawal method for most users, though the funds take 1-2 business days to arrive in your bank account. Same-day withdrawals at R35 use Luno's immediate payment rail and should arrive within hours on business days.

Bitcoin network fees are separate from Luno's platform fees. When you withdraw Bitcoin from Luno to an external wallet (not a Luno-to-Luno transfer), you pay a network fee which varies based on how congested the Bitcoin blockchain is at the time. Luno estimates this fee dynamically and displays it before you confirm the withdrawal. In practice, network fees for a standard Bitcoin withdrawal typically range from R15 to R60 depending on network conditions, making it economical to withdraw larger amounts less frequently rather than small amounts frequently.

Storing Your Bitcoin – Luno Wallet vs Hardware Wallet

Luno provides a built-in Bitcoin wallet that is custodial — meaning Luno holds the private keys on your behalf. For new investors buying small amounts (under R5,000), the Luno wallet is perfectly adequate and offers the convenience of immediate access through the app. Your Bitcoin is insured by Luno's custody arrangements, and you can access it from any device where you're logged into your account.

However, for amounts exceeding R10,000, financial experts universally recommend using a hardware wallet. Hardware wallets store your private keys offline, making them immune to phishing attacks and hacking attempts that could compromise software-based wallets. The two most popular hardware wallet brands available in South Africa are the Trezor Model T and the Ledger Nano X, both available from local retailers such as takealot.co.za and nearby electronics stores.

The Trezor Model T costs approximately R3,500 and features a touchscreen interface, while the Ledger Nano X retails for around R2,800 and connects via Bluetooth to the Ledger Live mobile app. Both devices support Bitcoin and hundreds of other cryptocurrencies. Setting up either device involves generating a 24-word recovery seed, writing it down on the provided card, and storing that seed in a secure location (not on your computer or phone). If you ever lose your hardware wallet, the recovery seed allows you to restore access to your funds on any compatible wallet.

Common Beginner Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most frequent mistake new Luno users make is buying Bitcoin during price spikes without a plan. Seeing the BTC/ZAR price surge by 10% in a day and rushing to buy at the peak is a recipe for disappointment when the inevitable correction arrives. Before buying anything, decide how much you want to invest, what price you're comfortable paying, and whether you're investing for the short term or the long term.

Another common error involves neglecting to complete FICA verification before attempting large deposits. Luno imposes holding periods and withdrawal restrictions on unverified accounts, causing delays that can be frustrating. Complete your verification before making your first deposit.

Finally, many new South African investors underestimate the impact of rand weakness on their Bitcoin returns. When the ZAR weakens against the USD, BTC/ZAR prices rise even if global BTC/USD prices remain flat. This means your returns from Bitcoin have two components: global crypto market performance and local currency movement. Understanding this dynamic helps you evaluate your portfolio performance more accurately and avoid blaming Bitcoin for currency-related losses that reflect rand depreciation rather than crypto market moves.
