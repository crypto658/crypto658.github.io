Crypto Tax South Africa 2026 — What SARS Requires You to Declare

April 17, 2026

Last updated: Loading...

The South African Revenue Service (SARS) has progressively clarified its position on cryptocurrency taxation over the past several years, and the rules for 2026 are now well-established — even if they remain poorly understood by many investors. If you have been trading Bitcoin on Luno, investing in Ethereum through Valr, or holding any cryptocurrency that you have sold, swapped, or given away in the past tax year, you likely have a reporting obligation. This guide explains exactly what SARS expects from South African crypto investors and how to stay compliant in 2026.

Does SARS Actually Tax Cryptocurrency?

Yes. SARS treats cryptocurrency as assets of an intangible nature for tax purposes. This means that when you dispose of cryptocurrency, you may trigger a taxable event that must be declared on your tax return. The key word here is disposal, which SARS defines broadly to include selling cryptocurrency for fiat currency (ZAR), trading one cryptocurrency for another (e.g., BTC for ETH), using cryptocurrency to purchase goods or services, and giving cryptocurrency away as a gift. Simply holding cryptocurrency does not trigger a tax event.

SARS classifies crypto gains as either capital gains or ordinary income depending on the circumstances of the disposal. The distinction is important because it determines which tax rate applies and how you calculate your liability.

Capital Gains Tax vs Ordinary Income — Which Applies to You

Capital Gains Tax (CGT) applies when you dispose of cryptocurrency as an investment asset held for personal enjoyment or long-term appreciation. This is the scenario that applies to most South African investors who buy Bitcoin and hold it for months or years. When you sell Bitcoin that you bought and held, any profit is treated as a capital gain and is included in your annual tax calculation at your marginal income tax rate, subject to the annual CGT exclusion.

The annual CGT exclusion for individuals in South Africa is R40,000 — meaning the first R40,000 of your total net capital gains across all assets in a tax year is exempt from tax. Any gains above that amount are added to your taxable income and taxed at your marginal rate. If you are in the 18% tax bracket, your effective CGT rate is 18% of gains above the threshold. If you are in the highest bracket (45%), your CGT rate is 18% of gains above the threshold after applying the inclusion rate (currently 40% of the gain is included in taxable income for individuals). Confusingly, for individuals the effective CGT rate works out to a maximum of 18% (45% marginal rate × 40% inclusion rate), not 45%.

Ordinary Income applies when cryptocurrency is disposed of as part of a trade or business activity. If you are actively day trading, running a crypto trading business, or providing crypto-related services for fees, your profits are treated as ordinary income and taxed at your full marginal income tax rate without the benefit of the CGT annual exclusion. This is a significantly higher effective tax rate for active traders and is the scenario that most closely mirrors how SARS treats income from traditional financial instruments traded as a business.

The line between investment and trading as a business is not always clear. SARS looks at factors such as the frequency of trades, the holding period of assets, whether you have a structured trading methodology, and whether trading represents your primary source of income. If you are executing dozens of trades per week and deriving your main income from them, SARS is more likely to classify your activity as a trade than if you execute one or two Bitcoin trades per month as part of a broader investment portfolio.

Disposal Events That Trigger Tax

Understanding what constitutes a disposal is fundamental to compliance. SARS identifies the following events as disposals for crypto assets:

  • Selling crypto for ZAR: Converting Bitcoin to South African Rand on Luno or any other exchange triggers a disposal. The gain or loss is measured as the difference between the ZAR proceeds received and your cost basis in the Bitcoin.
  • Trading one crypto for another: Swapping Bitcoin for Ethereum is a disposal event for CGT purposes even though you received no ZAR. Your Bitcoin is deemed to have been sold at market value, and the ETH you receive becomes your new cost basis in that asset.
  • Using crypto to pay for goods or services: If you pay for a holiday accommodation or a car using Bitcoin, the transaction is treated as a disposal of the Bitcoin at its market value on the date of the transaction.
  • Gifting cryptocurrency: Giving Bitcoin to a family member is a disposal event for the giver, though gifts between spouses are generally rollover transactions that do not immediately trigger CGT.

How to Calculate Your Crypto Gains for SARS

Calculating your crypto capital gain requires three pieces of information for each disposal: the proceeds (ZAR value of what you received), the base cost (ZAR value of what you paid for the cryptocurrency), and the date you acquired it. The gain is proceeds minus base cost. If the result is negative, you have a capital loss that can be set off against other capital gains — but cannot be set off against ordinary income.

For example, suppose you bought 0.5 Bitcoin on Luno in March 2024 for R400,000 total (R800,000 per BTC). In January 2026, you sold 0.25 BTC at a price of R2,100,000 per BTC, receiving R525,000 in ZAR. Your cost base for the 0.25 BTC is R200,000 (0.25 × R800,000). Your capital gain is R525,000 minus R200,000 = R325,000. After subtracting the R40,000 annual CGT exclusion, you have R285,000 of net capital gains to include in your taxable income. At an 18% marginal rate, your CGT liability would be R285,000 × 18% = R51,300.

SARS requires you to use the First-In-First-Out (FIFO) method when calculating cost base for crypto assets, unless you can demonstrate a specific identification method for your holdings. Most exchange platforms, including Luno, provide transaction histories in CSV or PDF format that you can use to reconstruct your cost basis position.

DeFi, Staking, and NFTs — What SARS Says

Decentralised finance protocols and staking rewards add complexity to tax calculations. In South Africa, rewards received from staking Ethereum or other proof-of-stake tokens are generally treated as ordinary income at the time they are received, measured at their ZAR market value on receipt. Your cost basis in the newly received tokens becomes the market value on the day of receipt. When you later sell those tokens, any gain above that cost basis is subject to CGT.

Yield farming rewards, liquidity pool tokens, and other DeFi-derived assets follow the same principle — they are income when received. The difficulty for South African investors is that many DeFi protocols do not provide the kind of transaction records that Luno or Binance SA offer, making record-keeping the investor's responsibility. This is where tools like Coinfinder or the services of a crypto-specialist tax practitioner become valuable.

NFTs are treated as digital assets for SARS purposes, subject to the same disposal rules as cryptocurrency. Creating and selling an NFT, or flipping NFT holdings for a profit, triggers a capital gains or ordinary income event depending on whether the activity constitutes a trade.

Record-Keeping Requirements

SARS requires taxpayers to maintain records for five years from the date of a tax return submission. For crypto investors, this means keeping the following documentation for every transaction: the date and time of the trade, the type of cryptocurrency involved, the quantity traded, the ZAR value of the cryptocurrency at the time of the trade (or the exchange rate used if calculating from a non-ZAR price), the ZAR proceeds of the transaction, and the fees paid. Exchange statements from Luno, Binance SA, Valr, or BitPads serve as primary records, supplemented by wallet records if you hold assets outside of exchange custodians.

SARS has the authority to request records during an audit, and failure to produce adequate records is an offence that can result in penalties. It is far better to maintain clean records than to face a SARS audit without documentation to support your tax positions. Most South African exchanges allow you to download full transaction histories in CSV format, which makes building a comprehensive record straightforward if you do it quarterly.

The Voluntary Disclosure Programme

If you have previous tax years where you earned crypto gains and did not declare them, SARS's Voluntary Disclosure Programme (VDP) offers a path to correct your position without facing the full penalties that would normally apply to non-compliance. The VDP allows you to voluntarily disclose outstanding tax liabilities and pay the correct amount with reduced penalties. SARS has extended the VDP to cover crypto-related tax obligations, and the programme has no fixed deadline, though SARS reserves the right to close it for specific categories of non-compliance at any time.

To make a VDP application, you need to engage a registered tax practitioner or submit directly through SARS eFiling. The disclosure must include full details of the omitted income or gains, the years involved, and the reasons for the non-disclosure. SARS will respond within 90 days, and once the VDP agreement is finalised, you are protected from prosecution for those periods.

When to See a Tax Practitioner

If you have traded extensively during a tax year — more than 20 transactions across multiple platforms — the complexity of your record-keeping and calculation burden justifies engaging a professional. Tax practitioners who specialise in cryptocurrency in South Africa include those registered with SAIT (South African Institute of Tax Professionals) who have direct experience with crypto asset taxation. A practitioner will typically charge between R1,500 and R5,000 for a crypto tax return preparation depending on complexity, which is money well spent if it saves you a SARS audit and penalties on a materially large gain.
